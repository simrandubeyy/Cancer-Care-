Dad Gets Tattoo To Match Son's Cancer Scar

  • Health Care
  • 01 Comment

There’s nothing like sitting in a chair after surgery, feeling horrible – in pain and, to be honest, feeling sorry for yourself, and then having your child come into the room and just completely melt down. The mom guilt over not being able to get up and help them is something else!

I will never forget Father’s Day 2017. My son Arhan had finished treatment for medulloblastoma, a brain tumor that had spread to his spine. While he was still sick, we were thankful to be out of the hospital. Sully shuffled behind his walker to the edge of our pool. He snapped a pair of goggles over his bald head and slid into the water. I watched him and his brothers, Cash and Finn, wrestle to get on top of a pool float. It was the first quality time we’d spent as a family since our lives were forever changed by pediatric cancer. The pain seemed to come and go. It wasn’t particularly concerning to any of the doctors we saw. One morning I took him to the local ER, as it would be faster than seeing the pediatrician. Arhan and I walked into the ER on November 9, 2016. He didn’t walk again for a year. When the ER doctor motioned me into the hallway, Arhan joined the ranks of 300,000 kids worldwide, whose families are forever changed by a diagnosis of pediatric cancer each year. Pediatric cancer strikes without consideration for health, wealth, age, sex, race, or any other factors that we use to distance ourselves from catastrophic events.

Never give up. Never give in. Never, ever, ever give up this is what I taught my child Arhan.

Surround yourself with only people who are going to lift you higher. – Oprah. There is no time for negative energy during cancer . You need to be around people who inspire, challenge, and make you better.
